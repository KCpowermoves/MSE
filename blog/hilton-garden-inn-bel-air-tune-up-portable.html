How Hilton Garden Inn Bel Air Captured $3,943 in BGE Rebates With a Whole-Property HVAC Tune-Up

April 2026 6 min read

If you have ever stayed at a hotel and silently graded the room temperature within the first thirty seconds of walking in, you already understand why HVAC matters in hospitality. Guests do not write reviews about the air conditioning when it works. They absolutely write reviews when it does not.

That makes HVAC one of the quietest revenue drivers a hotel has, and one of the most expensive things to get wrong. Which is exactly why the team at Hilton Garden Inn Bel Air decided to put every conditioning system in the property through the BGE Building Tune-Up program in one coordinated engagement.

The result was a comprehensive tune-up that captured $3,943.60 in BGE rebates, projected 53,792 kWh of annual energy savings, and reset the property's HVAC fleet to peak operating condition without any out-of-pocket cost to the operator.

Why a Hotel Is the Perfect Tune-Up Candidate

Hilton Garden Inn Bel Air opened in 2022 as a 102-room property along the I-95 corridor, drawing business and leisure travelers connected to Aberdeen Proving Ground, Ripken Stadium, and downtown Bel Air. The property runs every system, every hour, every day. There is no overnight setback. There is no holiday closure. Comfort is the product.

That is also exactly what makes a tune-up so valuable. When a system runs 8,760 hours a year instead of the 2,500-3,500 hours of a typical office, even small efficiency drift adds up to real money on the energy bill, and to real risk of an unexpected failure during a sold-out night.

The Hilton Garden Inn engagement covered eight separate HVAC systems serving the full property: in-room conditioning across guest floors, dedicated systems for back-of-house spaces, and rooftop units serving the lobby, fitness center, and meeting rooms. Each one was tuned up to the BGE Building Tune-Up specification and individually documented for the rebate.

What the Tune-Up Covered

The BGE Building Tune-Up program is built around a structured scope of work designed to restore a system's original operating efficiency without replacing equipment. For each of the eight systems at Hilton Garden Inn Bel Air, that included:

  • Coil cleaning across every system, using BGE-approved methods to restore heat-transfer efficiency on both heating and cooling cycles.
  • Refrigerant charge verified on every unit so each system runs at its design specification rather than working harder to achieve setpoint.
  • Air filters serviced to protect indoor air quality for guests and reduce strain on every blower motor in the property.
  • Eight nameplate-level documentation packages filed with BGE so every unit was fully credited to the rebate.
  • Zero impact on guest experience. Work was sequenced room by room, system by system, around the property's occupancy schedule. No room nights were lost to maintenance.

To put 53,792 kWh in context, that is roughly the annual electricity use of five average Maryland households. The hotel reclaimed that volume of consumption without buying a single new piece of equipment, and without disrupting a single guest stay.

Winter peak demand also dropped by 29.1 kW, which matters for a property that serves cold-weather travelers heading to Aberdeen Proving Ground or downtown Bel Air through the I-95 corridor. Heating capacity that was being burned through inefficient operation is now back in reserve.

Why this matters for hotel operators: Hospitality is one of the highest-runtime building types in any utility's portfolio, which means the energy savings from a tune-up compound faster here than almost anywhere else. The BGE Building Tune-Up program is structured so the rebate fully covers professional service for most properties. For a 102-room hotel, that often means a no out-of-pocket investment in equipment longevity and guest comfort.

Comfort Is the Product

Hotel general managers know what facilities managers in other building types sometimes forget: HVAC is not infrastructure, it is the product. A guest who is too cold or too warm at 2 a.m. is a guest who reaches for the phone, the front desk, and eventually the review keyboard. The cost of an HVAC issue in hospitality is measured in lost room nights, switched-loyalty travelers, and reputation, not just in kilowatt-hours.

That is what makes a structured, whole-property tune-up so worth doing. It is one of the few maintenance investments that protects guest experience, brand standards, equipment longevity, and the energy bill all at the same time. When a utility program like EmPOWER Maryland is also picking up the cost, the only real question is when to schedule it.
