Maryland BEPS Explained: What Building Owners Must Do Before the Deadlines Hit

April 1, 2026 8 min read

Maryland's Building Energy Performance Standards — BEPS for short — are now in full effect for large commercial and multifamily buildings across the state. If your building is 35,000 square feet or larger, the clock is already running. Many owners don't realize they're subject to BEPS until a compliance deadline is weeks away, at which point the available options shrink considerably.

This guide breaks down what BEPS actually requires, who it applies to, what the real deadlines are, and the concrete steps you should take right now to avoid penalties and position your property for long-term compliance.

What Is Maryland BEPS?

Maryland's Building Energy Performance Standards were established under the Climate Solutions Now Act of 2022 — one of the most ambitious state climate laws in the country. The law directed the Maryland Department of the Environment (MDE) to develop energy performance standards for existing buildings, with the goal of dramatically reducing the carbon footprint of Maryland's built environment.

BEPS works differently from traditional energy codes, which only apply to new construction or major renovation. BEPS applies to existing buildings that are already standing and in operation. It doesn't mandate a specific technology or upgrade — instead, it sets a performance target based on your building's energy use intensity (EUI), measured in kBtu per square foot per year.

BEPS doesn't tell you how to run your building — it tells you how well your building must perform. That distinction matters, because it gives owners flexibility in how they meet the standard.

The standard is benchmarked against similar building types using ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager, the U.S. EPA's free online tool for tracking building energy and water consumption. Maryland's BEPS targets are set as a percentage improvement from a building-type median, tightening over successive compliance cycles.

Who Does BEPS Apply To?

The current BEPS framework applies to buildings that are 35,000 square feet or larger. This covers a wide range of property types, including:

  • Office and professional buildings
  • Retail and mixed-use commercial properties
  • Multifamily residential buildings (5+ stories or meeting the sq ft threshold)
  • Hotels and lodging facilities
  • Healthcare and medical office buildings
  • Warehouses and light industrial facilities
  • K–12 schools and higher education buildings
  • Government-owned facilities

Some property types have modified timelines or alternative compliance pathways — notably certain agricultural facilities, religious organizations, and properties with specific operational constraints. If your building type is unusual, consult MDE's published guidance or speak with an authorized compliance advisor before assuming you're exempt.

How BEPS Compliance Actually Works

Compliance is structured around two obligations that work in tandem: annual benchmarking and meeting a performance target by the end of each compliance cycle.

Step 1: Annual Benchmarking

Every covered building must be benchmarked annually in ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager and report that data to MDE. Benchmarking involves entering 12 full months of energy consumption data — electricity, natural gas, fuel oil, steam, and any other energy sources — along with basic building characteristics like square footage, occupancy hours, and number of occupants.

Portfolio Manager then calculates your building's ENERGY STAR score (1–100) and your energy use intensity (EUI). These metrics form the baseline MDE uses to determine whether your building meets the BEPS target for its property type.

Annual reporting is not optional — it's a standalone compliance requirement even if your building already meets the performance threshold. Missing the reporting deadline can trigger penalties regardless of how efficient your building actually is.

Step 2: Meeting the Performance Target

Each compliance cycle spans multiple years and ends with a defined performance checkpoint. At each checkpoint, buildings must demonstrate that their EUI has improved to meet or beat the target set by MDE for their building type. Targets are based on a percentage reduction from the median EUI for comparable buildings nationally.

The targets get stricter with each successive cycle, driving continuous improvement rather than a one-time fix. Buildings that meet the current cycle's target are essentially banking compliance credit for the next cycle, giving them more runway to plan future upgrades.

Important: Simply benchmarking your building does not mean you're BEPS compliant. Compliance requires both annual reporting AND meeting the applicable performance target by the compliance cycle deadline.

Maryland BEPS Key Dates

The compliance timeline has several layers, which is where most building owners get confused. Here's a simplified breakdown of the major milestones:

Deadline Requirement
June 1, annually Annual benchmarking report due to MDE via ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager for the prior calendar year
2025–2027 cycle First BEPS performance cycle: buildings must demonstrate progress toward or achievement of Cycle 1 EUI targets
2028–2030 cycle Second compliance cycle with stricter targets; Cycle 1 non-compliant buildings face escalating penalties
2031+ Targets continue tightening toward net-zero-aligned performance levels through 2040

MDE has published interim guidance and may adjust specific target values before each cycle is locked in — which is another reason to stay current with official BEPS rulemaking rather than relying solely on third-party summaries (including this one).

What Are the Penalties for Non-Compliance?

Maryland BEPS carries real financial consequences. Buildings that miss the annual reporting deadline or fail to meet the performance target by the end of a compliance cycle are subject to penalties assessed by MDE. Penalty structures under the Climate Solutions Now Act framework include:

  • Reporting violations: Fines per day of non-reporting, which can accumulate quickly if a building owner ignores multiple notice cycles.
  • Performance violations: Per-square-foot penalties for each year a building fails to meet the applicable EUI target after a compliance cycle ends.
  • Repeat violations: Escalating penalty multipliers for buildings that remain non-compliant across consecutive cycles.

For a 50,000-square-foot office building, even modest per-square-foot penalties can add up to five-figure annual fines — costs that far exceed the price of the energy upgrades that would have brought the building into compliance in the first place.

5 Things Building Owners Should Do Right Now

1. Confirm Whether Your Building Is Covered

Pull your building's square footage from your certificate of occupancy, lease documents, or local assessor records. If you're at or above 35,000 square feet — or close to it — treat your building as covered and verify with MDE's official guidance. It's far better to benchmark a building that turns out to be slightly under threshold than to skip benchmarking one that's clearly covered.

2. Set Up ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager

If your building isn't already in Portfolio Manager, create an account at energystar.gov and add your property. You'll need 12 full months of energy data to generate a valid ENERGY STAR score and EUI. Contact your utility or request data sharing directly through Portfolio Manager — most Maryland utilities (BGE, Pepco, Delmarva Power, and SMECO) support automated data sharing for compliance purposes.

3. Find Out Where You Stand Against the Target

Once your building is benchmarked, compare your EUI against the BEPS target for your property type. MDE's published target tables show the required EUI threshold for each building category. If you're already at or below the target, your primary obligation is annual reporting — no upgrades required for this cycle. If you're above the target, you need a plan.

4. Prioritize High-ROI Efficiency Upgrades First

Not all efficiency investments move the needle equally on EUI. For most Maryland commercial buildings, the largest opportunities are HVAC optimization (often the single biggest energy system in the building), lighting upgrades (high-ROI, fast payback), and building envelope and controls improvements. The good news: EmPOWER Maryland rebate programs currently cover up to 85% of the cost for many of these upgrades, dramatically reducing the out-of-pocket investment required to reach compliance.

5. Document Everything

Keep records of all energy audits, upgrade invoices, contractor reports, and utility bills. If MDE audits your compliance submission or you need to demonstrate a good-faith improvement plan, documentation is your protection. A building that can show a clear trajectory of investment and improvement — even if it hasn't yet reached the EUI target — is in a much stronger position than one with no paper trail.

Key insight: Many of the upgrades that help you meet BEPS targets are already rebate-eligible under EmPOWER Maryland's current funding cycle. BEPS compliance and utility incentive programs are not in conflict — they're designed to work together. Use the rebates to fund the upgrades the standard requires.

Alternative Compliance Pathways

MDE has established limited alternative compliance pathways for buildings that face genuine structural barriers to meeting the standard through traditional energy upgrades. These pathways include:

  • Energy Audit and Improvement Plan: Buildings that cannot yet meet the target may submit a MDE-approved improvement plan demonstrating a credible roadmap to compliance, which can defer penalties during active implementation.
  • Hardship Provisions: Buildings with documented financial hardship or unusual operational constraints may apply for extended compliance timelines. These are evaluated case-by-case and are not guaranteed.
  • Historic Properties: Buildings listed on the National Register of Historic Places or subject to preservation easements that restrict energy upgrades may qualify for modified compliance standards.

These pathways are exceptions, not loopholes. They require formal applications, third-party documentation, and MDE approval — and they don't eliminate the obligation to improve, they adjust the timeline. Building owners who assume they can use hardship provisions as a substitute for compliance planning often find they don't qualify when the time comes.
