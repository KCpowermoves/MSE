What's New for Maryland BEPS in 2026: The HB 49 Changes Every Building Owner Must Know

April 3, 2026 6 min read

Maryland's Building Energy Performance Standards have been in development since the Climate Solutions Now Act passed in 2022, and the program has already seen meaningful legislative refinements. The most significant recent changes came through House Bill 49 (HB 49), which took effect in 2026 and introduced several important updates — including a new annual fee, a county-level waiver, and new exemption pathways for specific facility types.

If you own or manage a covered building in Maryland, these updates directly affect your obligations, your costs, and — in some cases — whether you're subject to BEPS at all. Here's a close look at each change and what it means in practice.

What's New for 2026 Under HB 49

HB 49 amended the Climate Solutions Now Act framework in several targeted ways. None of the changes eliminate the core BEPS benchmarking and performance obligations for most buildings — but they clarify who pays what, who is excluded, and how certain facility types will be treated going forward.

New Fee
Annual Reporting Fee: $100 Per Building

Starting in 2026, building owners will pay an annual reporting fee of $100 per covered building, adjusted for inflation going forward. This fee applies to every building subject to BEPS requirements — meaning every property at or above the 35,000 square foot threshold that is required to benchmark and report energy data annually to MDE.

The fee is modest relative to the cost of compliance work itself, but it's a real administrative obligation. Owners of multiple covered properties should expect this to aggregate — a portfolio of ten covered buildings, for example, would face $1,000 or more in annual fees before inflation adjustments.

The fee is expected to support MDE's administration of the BEPS program, including data review, enforcement, and technical assistance resources. Keep in mind this is separate from any penalties for non-compliance — it applies regardless of whether your building meets the performance target or not.

Waiver
Montgomery County Buildings: Waived from State BEPS

This is arguably the most consequential change for building owners in the DC suburbs. Buildings located in Montgomery County are now waived from Maryland's state BEPS requirements — including both the benchmarking and reporting obligations and the energy performance standards.

The reason: Montgomery County has its own Building Energy Performance Standards program that the state has determined is sufficiently rigorous to substitute for the statewide framework. Rather than requiring Montgomery County buildings to comply with two overlapping programs, HB 49 formally recognizes the county program as equivalent and exempts those buildings from state jurisdiction.

If you own property in Montgomery County, this does not mean you're off the hook — it means you need to be tracking and complying with the county's BEPS program, not MDE's program. Montgomery County's requirements have their own reporting deadlines, performance targets, and enforcement mechanisms, which may differ from the state program in meaningful ways. Verify your obligations directly with Montgomery County's Department of Environmental Protection.

Exemption
Hospitals: Eligible to Apply for Total Exemption

Hospitals present a genuinely difficult compliance challenge under BEPS. They operate 24/7, run energy-intensive clinical equipment, maintain strict temperature and air quality standards for infection control, and often cannot reduce energy consumption the way a typical office building or retail property can without compromising patient care.

HB 49 acknowledges this reality: hospitals are now eligible to apply for total exemption from BEPS requirements. This is an application-based process — exemption is not automatic, and hospitals will need to demonstrate to MDE why the standard cannot be reasonably met given their operational constraints.

Hospital administrators and facility managers who believe their property qualifies should begin the exemption application process early. MDE will likely require documentation of energy systems, clinical operations, and a demonstration that efficiency improvements are constrained by regulatory or patient-safety factors — not simply that upgrades would be expensive.

Exemption
SCIFs: EUI Target Exemption and Benchmarking Flexibility

Sensitive Compartmented Information Facilities — SCIFs — are purpose-built spaces designed to prevent electronic eavesdropping and information leakage. By their nature, they have specific structural, ventilation, and electrical requirements that can conflict with standard energy efficiency measures. They also cannot permit the kind of third-party access that energy audits and benchmarking typically require.

Under HB 49, SCIFs are eligible to apply for exemption from EUI performance targets and receive additional flexibility around benchmarking requirements. Like the hospital exemption, this is application-based — SCIF operators will need to document the facility's classified status and explain the operational constraints that make standard BEPS compliance impractical.

For building owners who have SCIFs as a portion of a larger covered property, the exemption may apply to the SCIF footprint specifically. How MDE calculates blended compliance obligations for mixed-use properties with SCIF components is something to clarify directly with the agency.

These four changes — the annual fee, the Montgomery County waiver, the hospital exemption, and the SCIF exemption — represent the most significant updates to Maryland BEPS since the program's initial rollout. For most commercial building owners, the fee and the county waiver are the most immediately relevant.

What's Coming in 2030: New Exclusions and Credits

HB 49 also established changes to the BEPS framework that will take effect beginning in 2030. These are worth understanding now, particularly for building owners in healthcare, laboratories, and facilities with significant onsite renewable generation, because they may affect how you plan long-term energy investments.

Effective 2030 and Beyond
Upcoming Exclusions and Credits Under HB 49
  • Energy use associated with steam production for sterilization in healthcare, laboratories, and life sciences facilities will be excluded from EUI calculations — recognizing that sterilization is a non-negotiable clinical process, not a reducible energy load.
  • Energy used for emergency backup power generation in healthcare facilities, assisted living and nursing homes, laboratories, military buildings, and critical infrastructure will also be excluded from EUI and greenhouse gas emissions calculations.
  • Building owners with onsite renewable energy generation will be able to apply credits against their EUI standard — meaning solar panels, wind, or other onsite generation will directly offset the energy performance calculation, rewarding buildings that invest in clean generation.
  • Facilities that use onsite biomethane — methane captured from biological sources rather than extracted from fossil sources — will be able to apply that usage as a credit against the net direct emissions standard.

The 2030 exclusions reflect a more nuanced understanding of where energy use in complex buildings is truly discretionary versus where it is operationally mandated. They also create a meaningful incentive for onsite renewable investment — particularly relevant as solar costs continue to fall and Maryland expands its net metering framework.

What These Changes Mean for Your Compliance Strategy

For most Maryland commercial building owners, the practical takeaways from HB 49 are:

  • Budget for the $100 annual fee per covered building starting this year. It's a small cost, but not accounting for it is an unnecessary oversight for a compliance-conscious portfolio.
  • If you're in Montgomery County, confirm your obligations under the county's BEPS program. The state waiver does not mean no obligations — it means different obligations, administered at the county level.
  • If you operate a hospital, evaluate whether applying for total exemption makes sense for your facility. The application process will take time; don't wait until a compliance deadline is approaching.
  • If your building includes a SCIF, document that now and begin the exemption application process with MDE. The classification requirements for SCIFs may require coordination with your security officer and facility management team.
  • If you're planning renewable energy investments, the 2030 onsite generation credit is a real incentive to act before that deadline — buildings that already have solar or other onsite generation in place will benefit from the credit immediately when the framework takes effect.

Note: Exemption eligibility under HB 49 — for hospitals and SCIFs — requires a formal application to MDE. Eligibility is not automatic based on building type alone. MDE has not yet published final guidance on the application process as of this writing; check MDE's BEPS program page for updates.
