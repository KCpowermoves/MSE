Maryland BEPS Third-Party Verification: The Every-Five-Years Rule Building Owners Can't Miss

February 17, 2026 7 min read

Most Maryland commercial building owners are now familiar with the annual benchmarking ritual under the Building Energy Performance Standards (BEPS) program: pull twelve months of whole-building energy data, enter it into ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager, and submit the report to the Maryland Department of the Environment (MDE) by the June 1 deadline. But tucked inside the BEPS rules is a second obligation that trips up a lot of owners because it only comes around once every five years — third-party verification.

Third-party verification is the independent audit of your benchmarking data. It's how Maryland makes sure the numbers building owners are self-reporting actually match reality, and it has real consequences if you skip it or fail it. Here's what the rule says, when it applies to your building, and how to prepare so the verification cycle doesn't catch you off guard.

What Third-Party Verification Actually Is

Under Maryland's BEPS regulations, every covered building — generally, commercial and multifamily properties 35,000 square feet or larger — must have its benchmarking report independently verified by a qualified third party on a five-year cycle. The verifier's job is to confirm that the data you entered in Portfolio Manager is accurate, complete, and matches the underlying utility bills, square footage, space-use characteristics, and operating profile of the building.

This isn't a light-touch review. A proper verification looks at the raw source data, compares it to what's been reported, checks for data gaps and reasonableness errors, and produces a signed letter or report attesting that the benchmarking submission is accurate. The result becomes part of your building's compliance record with MDE.

The idea behind third-party verification is simple: a performance standard is only as reliable as the data underneath it. If an owner's Portfolio Manager report shows strong energy use intensity (EUI) numbers but the underlying data is wrong — missing meters, the wrong gross floor area, misclassified space use — then neither MDE nor the owner actually knows where the building stands. Verification closes that gap.

The Five-Year Verification Schedule

Verification is tied to the BEPS reporting calendar. The first verification is due in 2026, covering calendar year 2025 data. A third-party verification is then required again in 2031 (covering 2030 data), in 2036 (covering 2035 data), and so on, every five years thereafter. Information required to be verified must be reported at least once every five years.

Following this schedule, verification repeats every 5 years.

The deadline falls on the same June 1 date as the annual benchmarking submission, so verification years effectively add a layer of work on top of the normal reporting flow. 2026 is the first year owners are feeling that in practice — and for many portfolios, it's the first exposure to a formal energy-data audit of any kind.

Who Qualifies as a Third-Party Verifier

MDE does not let just anyone sign off on a verification. The third-party verifier must be independent from the building owner and must hold recognized professional credentials demonstrating expertise in building energy systems and benchmarking practice. Typical qualifying credentials include:

  • Licensed Professional Engineer (PE) with experience in building energy analysis
  • Registered Architect (RA) with relevant building systems experience
  • Certified Energy Manager (CEM) through the Association of Energy Engineers
  • Building Energy Assessment Professional (BEAP) through ASHRAE
  • High-Performance Building Design Professional (HBDP) through ASHRAE

"Independent" is the key word. The person signing the verification cannot be an employee of the building owner, and generally cannot be the same person or firm that prepared the benchmarking report in the first place. The whole point of third-party review is that a fresh set of qualified eyes look at the data.

If you hire a consultant to do your annual benchmarking, plan on hiring a different qualified professional for the five-year verification. Using the same firm for both roles defeats the purpose of an independent audit and may not satisfy MDE's independence standard.

What the Verifier Actually Checks

A qualified verifier goes back to the source data and confirms that every key input in your Portfolio Manager report is accurate. Expect them to review the following elements at a minimum:

Verification Checklist
Core Data Elements a Verifier Will Examine
  • ✓Gross floor areaMeasured consistent with ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager definitions, including or excluding parking as appropriate.
  • ✓Property use typesEach space classified correctly with accurate use details — operating hours, occupancy, PCs, workers.
  • ✓Energy consumptionEvery fuel type captured for all twelve months: electricity, gas, fuel oil, district steam, propane, on-site renewables.
  • ✓Meter coverageAll meters serving the building are represented — no hidden tenant meters or gaps.
  • ✓Utility bill reconciliationEnergy consumption entered in Portfolio Manager ties back to actual utility bills from the serving utility.
  • ✓Weather normalizationSite EUI and weather-normalized source EUI calculations appear reasonable and error-free.
  • ✓Building characteristicsYear built, number of floors, and other descriptors match reality.
  • ✓Data quality flagsAny warnings or anomalies in Portfolio Manager are resolved or explained.

When verification uncovers errors — and it frequently does on the first pass — the benchmarking report must be corrected and resubmitted before the verification letter can be issued. This is why owners who wait until April or May of a verification year to start the process often miss the June 1 deadline. Identifying missing tenant meters, tracking down three years of missing fuel oil deliveries, or correcting a square footage error can take weeks.

Why Verification Matters Even If You're Already Compliant

Some owners assume that if their building is already hitting its performance target, verification is a formality. It isn't. Two issues come up regularly:

First, data errors cut both ways. Verification can reveal that a building was reporting worse performance than reality — for example, because a tenant meter was double-counted or a square footage figure was low. Owners who have been stressing about an "underperforming" building sometimes discover that the data was wrong and the property actually meets the standard. The opposite also happens: a verification reveals that a building thought to be compliant is actually over the limit once the errors are fixed.

Second, failure to submit a verification is itself a violation. Missing the five-year verification deadline exposes owners to the same non-compliance penalties as missing the annual benchmarking report — a separate obligation that can carry its own enforcement actions. MDE's authority to issue fines for BEPS non-compliance applies to both the annual reporting requirement and the periodic verification requirement.

How to Prepare for Your Verification Year

If 2026 is your first verification cycle — meaning your building is covered by BEPS and you reported 2025 data — now is the time to start, not May. A few practical recommendations:

  1. Pull your Portfolio Manager report and audit it yourself first. Check for data quality warnings, flat-lined meter months, and any spots where a reasonable reviewer would ask a question. Fix what you can before a verifier is involved.
  2. Gather source documents. Utility bills for all twelve months and all fuel types, building plans or rent roll showing gross floor area, and documentation of any space-use changes during the year.
  3. Engage a qualified verifier early. The pool of credentialed professionals in Maryland is finite, and the weeks before June 1 are their busiest. Booking in Q1 or early Q2 gets you better availability and more time to address findings.
  4. Budget for the fee. Verification is a separate professional service from annual benchmarking. Fees vary by building complexity and portfolio size, but owners should plan for a real line item — not an afterthought.
  5. Document what the verifier finds. Keep the signed verification letter, the corrected Portfolio Manager submission, and the verifier's working notes in your compliance file. If MDE audits your building later, this documentation is what they'll ask for.
